Problem Statement

Late diagnosis is the deadliest problem in gynaecologic oncology

Endometrial cancer is the most common gynaecologic malignancy in developed nations. Despite this, diagnostic workflows remain slow — relying heavily on manual radiologist review of CT and CT-PET scans that can take days to weeks.

When detected at Stage III or IV, five-year survival drops below 30%. Every day of diagnostic delay is clinically significant.

Current tools lack automated, AI-driven analysis pipelines that can simultaneously segment tumour volume, localise lesions, and classify malignancy risk — leaving clinicians without the decision support they need.

Understanding the Disease

What is Endometrial Cancer?

Endometrial cancer originates in the endometrium — the inner lining of the uterus. It is the fourth most common cancer in women in high-income countries, with incidence rising alongside obesity rates and an ageing population.

🧬

Biology

Most cases are Type I (endometrioid adenocarcinoma), driven by excess oestrogen unopposed by progesterone, often at lower grade. Type II (serous, clear cell) are more aggressive and harder to detect early.

⚠️

Risk Factors

Obesity, diabetes, late menopause, nulliparity, unopposed oestrogen therapy, hereditary Lynch syndrome, and PCOS are all associated with significantly elevated risk.

🔬

Current Diagnosis

Typically triggered by post-menopausal bleeding, confirmed via endometrial biopsy. CT and CT-PET imaging are used for staging — the key area where AI analysis can accelerate decisions.
